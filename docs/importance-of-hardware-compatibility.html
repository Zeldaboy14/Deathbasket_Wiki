The Importance of Hardware Compatibility

Contents

  1. 1 Things
  2. 2 Words
  3. 3 The Point
  4. 4 Q/A
  5. 5 Conclude
NB: I'm not writing this in response to anything, I just care a lot about the topic. I don't want anyone to read this and think I'm using it as a way to undermine their hacks or tools because that's really not my goal here. While some of this could be considered important for ROM hacking in general, it's geared towards N64 and Zelda because that's my background.

Things

    Hardware compatibility is something I consider to be vital in ROM hacking. It's also a large part of the reason I left the Zelda 64 scene: because it seems that so many people involved just couldn't care less about what the system they are developing for is actually capable of. I'm going to state the very basics that should be taken into account here:
  • ROM size should be 64MB at most. The biggest N64 cartridges available during its lifetime could hold 64MB of data. As far as I know, this is also the maximum size that flash cartridges can use. Simply put, anything over 64MB is already unrealistic for the system and doomed to not work on hardware. Many ROM modification projects I've seen have expressed plans to increase the ROM size beyond this.
  • RAM size is 4MB/8MB depending on whether or not the expansion pak is present. I've seen people talk about giving their game upwards of 40MB RAM and this is really just not feasible.
  • Graphics and models shouldn't be overly detailed. There's a reason N64 games are usually low-poly and low resolution and it's because the system will start to lag a lot if there's too much going on. Yeah, this is a little unfortunate, but if you're choosing to work with an N64 game then you should be prepared for this already.

Words

    The way I see it is that the casual newbie ROM hacker does not care about any of this. They just want to tinker with the game, throw a few numbers around, and see what the outcome is. This used to be me, too. They don't really know anything about the system's limitations or why certain things might or might not work on real hardware. I'll be the first to say that the lack of any good N64 emulators really doesn't help with this issue at all and is definitely a big part of the reason that many tools out there simply don't produce hardware compatible content. This article is mostly aimed at more involved people and project developers but it's good to remember that we all started somewhere.
    Anyway, like what seemed to be the majority of people involved with Zelda 64 at the time, I jumped straight into a big project (which was a terrible idea). I didn't really know what I was getting into and after a few months of randomly moving actors about, making small changes, and seeing the game crash hundreds of times without knowing why, it was a little frustrating. I guess that's when I took on learning assembly so that I could have more control over the game and understand it better. Of course, this is pretty much the roots of what has me writing this now.
    Quite soon after starting with assembly code and trying out various basic hacks, I was finding inconsistencies between emulators. I always saw this as an issue because if I were to release something, I'd want it to be as playable as possible. This is probably the first time I even considered hardware compatibility as a thing and it sucked to know that things might not work on console. I think the first instance I remember must have been from when I was developing a text-based interface in Ocarina of Time to replace parts of the HUD; it would crash on Project64 but display junk characters on the screen with Nemu64 while otherwise working normally. A similar thing happened with incorrectly calculated collision normals, which was a problem with older map importers that was known but went unfixed for quite some time. Many people in the community often had the attitude of "if it works on at least one emulator, it's good enough". I used to use Project64 as a gauge for what was good or bad since it was more picky, but that did not really work out either.

The Point

    Let's talk about now. It's 2015 and I left Zelda hacking maybe two or three years ago. I will admit that I still find it interesting, but looking at some things the community surrounding the game says and the way that many still treat the games (and each other) discourages me from really returning to it. I'm talking about people who liken hardware compatibility to an impossibility and even act like making their hack only work on emulators to "take advantage of their features" is something to be proud of. There are many awesome hacks of games like SM64 and OoT that have been/are in the works but there are very few that will be around forever or that will ever run on a console. This brings me to what I think is probably the most important point I want to make:
Emulators will eventually improve. And, when they do, your hack no longer works. Regardless of all the time and effort you may have put into it, it is now obsolete because it wasn't designed to work on the console it was... designed for. People who might have played these hacks will eventually not be able to as the more inaccurate emulators get phased out over time. Developing your ROM hack with hardware compatibility in mind guarantees that it will always be playable since, if it works on the real thing, any emulator worth your time will also run it.
    I can't stress the importance of the above statement enough. I already feel pretty disappointed that I can't take some hacks and play them on my console (in fact, I've heard my Boss Rush thing crashes somewhere so I should get on to fixing that someday). I get that not everyone has a flash cartridge they can test their game on but if you really want to do the best you can, you should consider getting one. It's a one-time purchase and honestly they are not that expensive. Regardless, communities should be working together to improve the tools they use so that all output eventually works on consoles, but rarely do I see this happen. Instead, it seems that people continue to use the same old tools with known issues and either forget about or just complain to the developers about hardware compatibility. Then there are projects like the infamous Ura Zelda project where they said it would only run on their custom emulator built specifically for the game. This is just awful practice and an example of something that should never have to happen. If you want to make an N64 game then make an N64 game, not something that only works under very specific conditions that almost nothing can replicate. If you're not okay with adhering to the limitations of the console's hardware, develop your game for something else instead of trying to pretend they don't exist. Honestly, I've felt for a while that Zelda 64 hackers have lost sight of what ROM hacking is really about and this is the reason that so many projects end up dying. I'm not saying this to insult the community, I really wish I had the motivation to go back and contribute.
    Also something I want to note here: I see people making changes in certain ROM hacks to help with issues that are known to only be caused by the emulator they are using. Never do this! I played someone's hack of Ocarina of Time a while ago and the pause screen was messed up because they had patched in a code that "fixes" it on emulators.

Q/A

  • Why should I care about the hardware if the game I'm starting from doesn't work on it anyway?
    • I've actually seen people saying this about the Ocarina of Time Master Quest debug version! I don't know where it came from, but this myth needs to disappear. This ROM works perfectly well on an N64 console (it was developed for and tested on one, after all). If you're working from somebody else's hack that is already broken, figure out why it doesn't work and fix it.
  • Nobody will play my hack on a console anyway, why should I care?
    • Nobody will play it on a console because you don't care. I can say from experience that there are a lot of ROM hacks I would have played if they worked on console, but I feel much less motivated to play on emulators. I know other people who have said the same things. Even if this is the minority of players, there are people who will be disappointed and miss out on playing your game because you didn't care enough about it to put any effort into making it work on its native hardware. Think about what I mentioned earlier: if your hack doesn't work on console, it doesn't work. Have you really achieved anything from your hours of time and effort spent on it in that case?
  • What can I do if I can't test on actual hardware? Surely I don't have a chance.
    • I made Majora's Mask Randomiser in 2013. I had no way to play it on console then but I've since confirmed that it does work. I tested this hack on a few emulators (Nemu64, Project64, Wii VC) and found some different bugs with each. For example, some graphics errors might be caught with one emulator or plugin but not another. Nemu64 has an option to show errors which I'd recommend turning on while testing as you might find something with it. It's good to remember that many things found like this will crash the N64 console but will not crash an emulator.
    • If you can play on a console or can have a friend do it for you, using a game's crash debugger is always a good idea (unsure if any progress was made on activating it in MQ debug yet).
    • If you start out with the idea that your hack won't work, you'll probably be less motivated to make sure it does.
    • One thing to be wary of is when an emulator is doing something weird with the game. I ran into this when testing on Wii VC - I couldn't get my code loaded in a certain area of RAM at all, even loading it from the ROM and immediately jumping to it did not work despite working on everything else.
  • But with emulation there are more possibilities for the game!
    • With accurate emulation there is not. Taking advantage of inaccuracy that prevents crashing or lag or that allows you to vastly increase ROM/RAM size is not the same as making a ROM hack. You have to work with the limitations you're given and if that bothers you then perhaps you'd be better suited to a different environment. Again: do you want to produce something that everybody can play, or something that only works under very specific conditions, relying on flaws in other programs, that's likely to get ignored by many people who would otherwise be interested?
  • [X] tool doesn't work with hardware so what's the point?
    • This is why being a community should be important - working together to make things better for everyone. Tools should be tested with hardware when possible and tweaked to work properly, but don't blame developers if they aren't complete yet. This can be achieved. Where it can't be tested, at least trying for output that is structured the same way as data already in the game should be a good start until someone is willing to get the results on hardware, even if this means having to pull apart and reconstruct files from the game to test if your processes are doing the right thing.

Conclude

    I'll conclude here for now. I'm not sure if this will ever even get read but I hope a made a few good points. What I want to be taken from this: if you make a game for system [x], make sure it works on system [x]. I'm tired of seeing ROM hacks not work on console and/or people seeming to think it's not possible to make it happen. If you go to the effort to make a hack in the first place, it deserves to stay playable and not be rendered irrelevant when a better emulator comes out and people can no longer play it. Ask yourself why you want to do this. For me, I always wanted to experience a game the way I first played it but with my own content. That's not how I feel about something that only works on an emulator.
